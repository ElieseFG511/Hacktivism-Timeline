Worst out for Best

Since the early 90’s hacktivism has been an integral part of the history of the internet, whether it would be declaring “cyberwars” on countries to creating the means to initiate Distribution Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks. With time, however, attacks have gotten more complex, risque, controversial, but have diminished within size and seem to be less effective than ever. This has all but faded into obscurity in the wake of authoritarian view almost, though that does seem to be speculation as that is what they do fight typically. Groups tend to be anarchistic in their design, not to cause chaos, but to distribute the power to as many as possible so that their cause may be larger than a city, state, country, or world.

The rise of groups such as Cult of the Dead Cow (cDc) still have active websites that have possibly haven’t changed since the early 90’s. Some groups, such as anonymous, have shut down their youtube pages, and have faded from the mainstream as powers have changed. Some simply remain legends and are meant to be, we shouldn’t fear or revere them, but we should know what they’ve done for us.

1990

Hong Kong Blondes

The fictional hacktivist group allegedly found holes within the Chinese internet system, specifically with the use of satellite communications. Their goal was to beat China’s firewall to destroy the censorship for the People’s Republic of China’s people, as well as target U.S. businesses that were partnering with China. Unknowingly, and allegedly, created by the United States Government.

1996

Department of Injustice

Unknown hackers got onto the Department of injustice, changing the title to the “Department of Injustice” putting explicit images in response to the “Communications Decency Act”. This law tried to restrict the access of pornographic materials on the internet, which after this attack backfired in the following year after a court ruling decided that was unconstitutional.p>

1998

Electronic Disturbance Theatre

Members of the EDT created FloodNet, which allows users to create DDoS attacks to help rebels in Chiapas. FloodNet took the form of a Java Applet and it allowed users to send absolutely useless requests or even personalized messages to the server. Slowing it down to a crawl and filling with error logs. This served as a “virtual Sit-in” which can cripple a targeted organization if they can’t handle the load.

1998

The December Cyberwar

An alliance between Cult of the Dead Cow (cDc), L0pht, Chaos Computer Club, Hacker Mags 2600 and Phrack rose into the public view. They called themselves the “Legion of the Underground”, and they declared a cyberwar against China and Iraq in retaliation to their numerous human right’s violations. There was criticism as this possibly impeded the free access to information, for the citizens of China.

2001

Hacktivismo Declaration

Hacktivismo (Sect of cDc) issued the document known as the Hacktivismo Declaration, which served and still serves as a code of conduct for those participating. Hacktivismo especially took the time to declare that their goal was to stop state-sanctioned censorship of the internet as well as working towards the freedom of opinion and expression.

2009

Iranian Elections

Anonymous widely spread information about 2009 Iranian elections, releasing a video manifesto and a website by the name of “Anonymous Iran” in order to push this information further.

2010

Avenge Assange

A coordinated, decentralised attack from Anonymous occurred to several credit unions, political adversaries, and even various companies. Companies such as Mastercard, Amazon, Visa, Paypal, and various others had cut off donations to the WikiLeaks site and attempted to shut the site down. However, the anarchistic hacktivist group used DDoS attacks to such companies which ranged in effectiveness from basically nothing to needing to be abandoned.

2011

Egypt's Internet Shutdown

In 2011 the government of Egypt shut down internet access for the whole country creating an information blackout to disrupt marches, and disrupt dissent. This obviously posed a threat to human rights.

2011

Google and Twitter

Google partnered with Twitter to create and release SayNow and “Speak to Tweet” which helped the Egyptian people communicate with each other. Twitter had set up designated lines where people can leave voicemails and those would be turned into tweets that would be sent onto the site in mass.

2011

Telecomix Dials

Provided dialup services and tech support to the people of Egypt, releasing a statement showing their support to the Egyptian people. They described their efforts in a video manifesto, describing how to avoid government surveillance online.

2013

Million Mask March

Anonymous crashed 30 government websites in the Philippines, posting a video on YouTube to gather people in front of the Parliament House to show the disdain they had for the Filipino government.

2014

Sony Hack

Sony Entertainment Pictures was hacked by the Guardians of Peace (GOP) who gathered over 100 Terrabytes of data, including unreleased films, employee salaries, Social Security data, passwords and account information. GOP went through several social media accounts changing the passwords to diespe123.
